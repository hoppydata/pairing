Welcome to 11th Edition! This week we’re taking a deep dive into every part of the new edition, from the new rules to the missions to how it’ll affect your armies. 
One of the biggest changes to 11th edition is the change to how missions work. A stark contrast to the 20-plus static missions of tenth edition, the missions of this new edition are dynamic, based on the Detachments - and by extension units - taken in each player’s army, and the actual objectives based on the combination of those two missions. In this article we’ll look at the new missions, how they work, and what you need to know about playing with them.


Before we dive in, we'd like to thank Games Workshop for providing us with a preview copy of these rules for review purposes.
Force Dispositions

Before we can talk about missions, we need to talk about Force Dispositions. When you build an army in 11th edition, you build it using Detachments. For players new to the game, Detachments are extra rules that benefit specific units in your army, giving you enhancements you can use to upgrade those units and Stratagems you can play during games to further improve those units by spending Command Points.

What’s new in 11th edition is that now those Detachments each have a Force Disposition, which is used to determine which types of missions your army can play. When building their army players may choose a Force Disposition for their army from among those associated with one of the Detachments they are using. So if you have two Detachments, one with Purge the Foe and another with Disruption, you could choose either of those to be your army’s Force Disposition.

Note that in the Core Rules, players choose Force Disposition before each game. In the tournament pack, this is done when mustering your army, and so players do not change Dispositions before each game.

There are five core Force Dispositions in 11th edition. They are:



Disruption

Priority Assets


Purge the Foe

Reconnaissance


Take and Hold


Each of these produces a different type of mission experience and will push players toward building a different type of army.
Disruption

Disruption missions focus on hit-and-run tactics, with a mix of action and objective play. Three of these missions - the missions in which you take on an opponent with Take and Hold, Reconnaissance, and Priority Assets - have actions associated with them. These missions push you toward having a number of fast, small, lethal units which can move around the table, do actions, and kill key enemy targets.
Priority Assets

By far the most action-driven of the missions, Priority Assets gives you a special action to perform in every single mission on top of holding objectives. This means that you’ll need lots of action units which can move quickly to key locations and do actions. You’ll also need those units to have decent OC, as completing those actions requires controlling the objectives, though they all complete at the end of your turn so you won't need to hold them through an entire round.
Purge the Foe

These are some of the most straightforward primary missions in the deck. They require you to kill enemy units and hold objectives. If you just want to play standard Warhammer, this and Take and Hold are the two mission types to choose. On our initial examination these appear to be the "strongest" missions, in that they're straightforward and reward you for doing the things that Warhammer armies already want to do during games, i.e. kill things and be in cover. 
Reconnaissance

Reconnaissance missions are all about getting around the table, either just being in every table quarter or performing actions in specific locations. These are primary missions where you want fast units which can show up, do an action, and leave. These missions let you score for holding an objective outside of your deployment zone each round, but are also likely to reward you for grabbing an objective in the first battle round. 
Take and Hold

Take and Hold missions are similar to Purge the Foe in that they have that “classic Warhammer” feel. There are no actions here - these primary missions purely reward you for holding objectives and are the only set which can reward you for holding your home objective. These are pretty straightforward as well, though they feel a bit disadvantaged up against Purge the Foe, which rewards you for holding objectives and killing things.
Primary Mission Selection

Once you know your Force Disposition, you can play an opponent. The missions you play will be decided by the pairing of your two Dispositions, meaning that you will be playing different primary missions, and there are fifteen different combinations you can end up with. Before the game you and the opponent will compare your Force Dispositions and the combination of those two will determine the primary missions you each get.
For example, if your army Disposition is Purge the Foe and your opponent’s is Priority Assets, then you’ll play the Destroyer’s Wrath Mission, while they’ll play Vital Link. But if you’re both on Purge the Foe, you’ll both play the Meatgrinder mission. 

Almost every primary mission rewards you for holding at least one objective outside your deployment zone starting in the second battle round, and many of them will ask you to kill enemy units, even when you aren’t in Purge the Foe. During a game, you can score up to 45 Victory Points from your primary mission, with a maximum of 15 VP in any given round. Most missions give the player going second the added benefit of scoring for holding objectives at the end of their turn on round 5 instead of in their Command phase.

Secondary Missions

Similar to tenth edition, players don’t just rely on primary missions for scoring; in addition to your primary mission you’ll also have secondary missions to choose from. At the start of the game you can choose between either Fixed or Tactical missions. Fixed missions gives you two secondary mission options, chosen from a list of four, while if you choose Tactical you’ll randomly draw two missions every round to score.
Fixed Missions

Your four fixed mission options are:


Assassination - score VP for killing enemy characters

Bring it Down - score VP for killing enemy units with 10+ wounds


A Grievous Blow - score VP for killing enemy units with a starting strength of 13+. If you played tenth you'll recognize this as a renamed Cull the Horde.

Engage on All Fronts - score VP for having units in three or four table quarters


You can score a maximum of 15 points for these in a given round, and a maximum of 20 VP from each Fixed secondary during a game. Because they offer a lower ceiling and less flexibility, you want to be very sure you’re going to max both of your choices if you take them. That’ll typically mean that they’re reserved for rare situations where both can be maxed out, such as if you’re playing against five large Imperial Knights, where you can score 4 VP for Assassination for each kill (3 VP +1 for having 4+ Wounds), and 4 VP for Bring it Down for each.
Tactical Missions

With Tactical Missions, you’ll draw two secondary missions from your missions deck in each of your Command phases, and each one has different criteria for how to score it. There’s a total of 18 secondary missions in the deck, and you can score a maximum of 15 VP in any round off secondaries, for a maximum of 45 VP during a game.
Unlike in 10th edition, you have no maximum hand size in 11th, so you’ll draw new Secondary Objectives each round to add to your existing ones, though you can still opt to discard missions at the end of your turn for Command Points. Missions can then be held for later in the game and scored on later turns. Over five rounds you’ll see ten missions, and most are worth 3-5 VP, so you’ll likely only be able to discard at most two before you start lowering your total scoring potential. 

Most of the secondary missions in the deck are going to be familiar to 10th edition players - secondary missions like A Tempting Target, Behind Enemy Lines, Cleanse, and Secure No Man’s Land are back with small tweaks. The new missions here are:



Forward Position is a reworked version of Capture Enemy Outpost, giving you 5 VP For holding either your opponent’s home objective or each Expansion objective. 

Plunder replaces Sabotage and can be redrawn if you already have Cleanse (and vice versa). It works a little differently, and has some weird language that will almost certainly need a clarification at some point to make it clear whether your unit or the terrain feature has to be in your opponent's territory.


Beacon lets you pick a unit on the battlefield or embarked in a Transport to be your Beacon unit and if they survive to the end of your opponent’s turn you’ll score VP if they’re outside your deployment zone.

A Grievous Blow just replaces Cull the Horde, asking you to kill units with a starting Strength of 13+.


Burden of Trust moves from primary to secondary, having you pick objectives on the battlefield and units to guard them. Keep those objectives guarded and you’ll score FP.

Centre Ground replaces Area Denial.


If you’re new to secondary missions, what you need to know is that each one will reward you with between 2 and 5 VP for accomplishing some specific task, such as holding two objectives in No Man’s Land, having units in all four table quarters, or completing an action on multiple objectives you control. Some of these are modal, giving you ways to score VP for completing them partially, while others are all-or-nothing. 

Secondary missions are a welcome return; they give players a lot of flexibility in scoring and reward playing armies which are versatile; elite armies focused purely on killing may find they’re dropping VP when it comes to objectives which require doing actions or being in specific locations. Even those armies, taking Purge the Foe as a primary, may still want one or two units around to do Actions so they can score secondary missions.

The changes to secondary missions also make them more important in 11th edition - being able to go up to 45 VP on them means they are now worth more of your score, and the ability to keep drawing them if you don’t score means you can stack them for later turns, making it less of a problem if you draw “bad” secondary missions early on. 

Final Thoughts

Despite the changes to how they’re selected, we suspect that Missions in 11th edition will largely feel familiar to tenth edition players. Your core focus in most games is still going to be holding objectives and killing enemy units, things you’ll just need to do in every game if you want to stop an opponent from scoring, though the changes in primary scoring are going to give things an interesting feel based on your army. 
In that sense, your army Disposition is going to have a huge impact on how you build your army and which units you take - and you can expect we’re going to write many, many articles on that topic in the future - but you’ll still need some versatility to account for secondary missions. In a similar sense, we expect that the Dispositions your Detachments give you access to will also have a major impact on your decision to take them. It’s an interesting wrinkle that promises to keep things interesting in 11th edition for years to come.
